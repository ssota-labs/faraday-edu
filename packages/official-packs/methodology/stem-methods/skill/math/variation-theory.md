# Variation theory — invariant amid deliberate change

**Variation theory** (Marton & Booth 1997; Watson & Mason) designs a **pattern of
examples** so learners discern what is invariant and what varies. You don't
"explain the theorem" first — you run a controlled sequence of cases until
structure pops.

## When to use

- Functions, proportionality, geometric relationships, algebraic identities
- "Same surface, different deep structure" discrimination (e.g. `(a+b)²` vs `a²+b²`)
- Secondary through undergrad concept refinement

## Unit arc

| Phase | Learner does | Block families |
|---|---|---|
| **Contrast** | Compare near-miss cases; predict which property holds | **Manipulative** or **data** (linked charts), **check** (prediction) |
| **Generalize** | State what stayed invariant across cases | **Formalism** (short), **narrative** |
| **Apply** | New case where only one variable changes | **Check** (numeric/symbolic), optional **executable** |
| **Boundary** | Case where the invariant breaks — limits, exceptions | **Manipulative**/**data**, **formalism** (`Reveal`) |

## Rules

- Vary **one meaningful dimension at a time** early; compound variation comes
  after the invariant is stable.
- Near-miss pairs beat random example soup.
- Spatial and data families often beat prose for contrast phases.

## Anti-patterns

- Listing examples without a prediction/commitment step between them.
- Variation without an invariant the learner is meant to discover.
