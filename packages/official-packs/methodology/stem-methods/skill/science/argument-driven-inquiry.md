# Argument-driven inquiry (ADI)

**Argument-driven inquiry** structures lab as: **question → investigation → claim,
evidence, reasoning → peer review → revise**. Learners defend claims with data,
not fill worksheets (Sampson et al.; NSTA ADI model).

## When to use

- Lab sciences where epistemic practice matters (chem, bio, geo)
- Secondary honors through undergrad intro labs
- When the goal includes *how we know*, not only *what we know*

## Unit arc

| Phase | Learner does | Block families |
|---|---|---|
| **Question** | Operationalize a research question | **Narrative** + **check** (what would we measure?) |
| **Investigation** | Collect or simulate data systematically | **Manipulative**, **data**, **executable** (sim/lab code) |
| **Argument** | Claim + evidence + reasoning | **Narrative** (structured template), **data** anchors |
| **Review** | Critique a sample argument or their own | **Check** (weakest link MCQ), **compare** family |
| **Revise** | Improved claim after feedback | **Narrative** or **check** submission gate |

## Rules

- Evidence must be **traceable** to learner-generated or provided datasets (charts,
  tables) — not authority prose alone.
- Peer review in solo courseware: use a **deliberately flawed sample argument**
  in the check family.
- CER (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) scaffolds stay short; fade across the course.

## Anti-patterns

- Investigation with predetermined "right graph" only.
- Argument phase that's a single-sentence conclusion with no reasoning chain.
